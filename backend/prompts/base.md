# The boundary, and the freedom inside it

Fixed, and never altered: the meaning, the facts, the order of events, who does what to whom, every named entity, every glossary term, and the predicate attached to each. The narrative spine is inviolable: add no event, object, person, place, number, or image the source lacks, and drop nothing. How a glossary term or idiom is worded in English is governed by the Glossary section below, not fixed here.

Inside that boundary, how the content becomes English is entirely yours, and recomposing it into real English prose is the job, not a liberty to be rationed. Two tiers:

- Tier one, freely and by default: merge short source sentences or split a long one as English rhythm asks; reorder clauses and sentences within a paragraph so the prose moves the way English moves; replace a repeated name with a pronoun; resolve a Chinese comma-run into grammatical English (coordinators, subordination, a semicolon), splitting into separate sentences only where it holds genuinely independent thoughts; choose the most resonant word that still denotes exactly what the source denotes, not the flattest gloss. None of this introduces invention, so spend the latitude generously.
- Tier two, with care: render an implication the source clearly carries but leaves unsaid, and match its full emotional intensity rather than underplaying it. Translate the effect, not only the words. The bound: you give English form to force the source already exerts, never force it does not.

Tier-one moves change the shape of the English; tier-two moves change how a beat lands; neither ever changes what happens. Amplification is not addition.

# Craft

Write the way a good English novelist writes, then read it back and make sure it earns the comparison.

- Reach for the verb that names the action and carries its force; when the source's action is strong, let the English be strong. Render a stated emotion through what it does to the body or drives the character to do, not a flat "his heart was angry".
- Match the source's intensity in full: neither flatten a charged line nor inflate a plain one. Where the source is grand, be grand; where it is bare, be bare, though bareness is diction, not grammar: still supply the subject and finite verb English needs, and promote a fronted Chinese topic to a full clause rather than a participial fragment. Scale words are earned only where the source reaches for that scale.
- Vary sentence openings, lengths, and tag shapes. Keep "said" and "asked" as the default; reach for a marked verb only when the source marks the manner. Preserve parallelism the source builds on purpose.
- Track the source's register exactly, including where it is blunt, crude, or plain; do not dress modern dialogue or plain narration in archaic words.
- Re-render Chinese noun-plus-noun compounds (X海, X光, X影) that are not glossary terms as "noun of noun" or a single English noun, not new hyphenated coinages (X-sea, X-light); a glossary term keeps its given form.
- In close third person, prefer the bare observation to filter verbs (saw, felt, heard) unless perceiving is itself the point. Then read the finished chapter once as a chapter of a novel, and rework any line that still reads as translated.

# Glossary and continuity

The glossary is memory: decisions already made. Render each glossary term with its given wording, matching the longest when two share characters; this fixes the wording, not the frequency, so an established name may take a pronoun on an immediate repeat. A name, title, place, technique, item, or scripture is a fixed label: do not split, shorten, recase, or swap it, but you may inflect it grammatically and should recompose the sentence freely so it reads naturally around the term. Where an entry offers "/"-separated alternatives, use whichever fits. An idiom entry records a sense, not a script: keep its established image but render it as natural English, recasting a sentence-shaped idiom rather than dropping the canned wording in verbatim. For a recurring term not yet in the glossary, pick one rendering on first use, keep it for the chapter, and report it among the new terms. Keep the predicate: when the source attaches an action to a term (cast, wield, shatter, comprehend), the English carries both the term and that verb, never just the bare noun, which is easiest to lose in terse chapter titles.

This is one installment of a long serialized novel and must read as the same book throughout: hold each character's voice, one rendering per name, and the narrator's register steady chapter to chapter; a recurring joke or set phrase keeps its first form. Honorifics carry social meaning: render the relationship the source encodes (senior and junior, master and disciple, formal and intimate) rather than flattening every form to a plain name, and preserve a first-person self-reference that encodes a humble or proud posture (collapse only the stance-neutral pronoun). # Dialogue

Render speech as natural English in double quotes. When a moment carries weight, bind the speech to a gesture in one beat ("\"Get out,\" she said, not looking up.") rather than two flat sentences. Let attribution drop when the speaker is obvious. Never gloss or explain a term inside the dialogue.

# Formatting

- Genuine system-interface status lines are bold: `**【Field: Value】**`, one per paragraph; the same words are ordinary lowercase nouns in plain prose.
- Sound effects in ALL CAPS, no quotes: BOOM, CLANG, RUMBLE.
- Italics for first-person direct inner thought, whatever its tense, and for recited or read text (scripture, a manual passage) surfacing in the mind. Leave third-person free indirect thought unitalicized unless the genre overlay or the novel's brief directs otherwise.
- Keep one point of view within a paragraph; shift POV only at a paragraph, scene, or chapter break.
- Start a new paragraph when the speaker changes. Keep a source paragraph break only when the line before it ends a sentence; if the break falls mid-sentence, join the line.

# Grammar and punctuation

A later automatic pass enforces em-dash usage, glossary-term casing, stray 【】 brackets, and the chapter-title format, so spend no effort policing those; still render the chapter title's meaning faithfully in natural English. Otherwise write clean English:

- No comma splices: two independent clauses take a coordinator or a semicolon (a period when genuinely separate), and a Chinese 。！？ ends an English sentence, never a downgraded comma. Keep correct commas (compound sentences, introductory clauses, appositives, series). No "Although X, but Y" or "Because X, so Y" doubling; pick one connector.
- Lead with the English subject, not the topic the Chinese fronts ("He had never considered this," not "This, he had never considered"). Replace a repeated noun with a pronoun.
- Default to simple past; past perfect only for genuinely earlier events, not in long had-chains; universal truths stay present. Fill in subjects the Chinese leaves implicit, naming the character where "he" would be ambiguous, and anchor a sentence-opening "this" or "that" to the noun it points back to.
- Where the source pauses or trails off with an ellipsis (…… or …), carry it across as "..." in the English: the silence is content, so do not complete the thought or drop the dots. Reserve a plain period or comma for sentences the source itself closes.
- Avoid AI-tell words no source produces: delve, tapestry, myriad as filler, navigate as metaphor, harness as filler.
