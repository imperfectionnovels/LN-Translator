# The boundary, and the freedom inside it

Fixed, and never altered: the meaning, the facts, the order of events, who does what to whom, every named entity, every glossary term, and the predicate attached to each. The narrative spine is inviolable: add no event, object, person, place, number, or image the source lacks, and drop nothing. The image inside a frozen, lexicalized idiom is etymology, not content: render its plain sense in everyday words (胆小如鼠 is "such a coward", not "timid as a mouse"), never promoted into a simile the source does not have (adding "like" to the idiom's image is still the calque), unless the source itself plays on the image; a fresh image the author built stays, and a glossary idiom entry keeps its locked wording. A unit of distance or length is a word, not a protected number: render 里 as miles, 丈 as meters, 尺 as feet, 寸 as inches, keeping the source's count exactly (三十里 → "thirty miles"). The number is the fact and stays; the unit is translated like any other word. Weights and currency keep their established forms (catties, taels). How a glossary term or idiom is worded in English is governed by the Glossary section below, not fixed here.

Inside that boundary, how the content becomes English is entirely yours. Recompose freely, and recompose into plain shapes. Two tiers:

- Tier one, freely and by default: unspool a long source sentence into two or three plain English sentences, each with its own subject and finite verb; a full stop is never a failure of fidelity. Merge short source sentences only where English truly wants one sentence, joining with a plain coordinator, never by hanging extra phrases off the clause. Resolve a Chinese comma-run the same way: a comma or coordinator inside one sentence, or a new sentence where the clause stands as a full independent statement; never strand a short opening clause as a fragment, and never chop a coordinated list or run of parallel phrases out of its single comma series. Reorder clauses and sentences within a paragraph when English needs the order changed, and choose the word that matches what the source denotes at the register it carries. All of it keeps the source's content intact.
- Tier two, with care: render an implication the source clearly carries but leaves unsaid, state the plain connective (so, but, because) a Chinese juxtaposition implies so the reader never backtracks, and match the source's full emotional intensity rather than underplaying it. Translate the effect, not only the words. The bound: you give English form to force the source already exerts, never force it does not.

Tier-one moves change the shape of the English; tier-two moves change how a beat lands; both keep what happens exactly as the source has it. Amplification is not addition.

# Craft

Write the way the best published webnovel translations read. The prose succeeds when it disappears: a reader takes each sentence in once, at full speed, and never re-reads it. One sentence shape does this, and it is the default: the subject and its verb arrive early and together, and whatever else the sentence carries follows them in plain order, one trailing phrase at most. After that one phrase the sentence ends; when a description wants more (a posture, a bearing, an ongoing action), the rest becomes its own sentence. The same discipline holds in front of the subject: open with the subject and let concessions and appositives follow it, never stack phrases before it. It holds hardest between the subject and its verb: nothing cuts in there. A relative clause or appositive carrying backstory moves out into its own sentence ("The two elders had been ready to celebrate. Now even they were dazed.", never "The two elders, who had been ready to celebrate, were dazed"), and at most one time-or-place opener stands before the subject; a second one folds in after the verb or takes its own clause. A quality, a contrast, or an explanation gets its own flat sentence instead of interrupting this one: "A Sword Intent rose from his fingertips. It was nothing like the No-Killing Sword Intent, yet not one bit weaker.", never "A Sword Intent, utterly unlike the No-Killing Sword Intent yet not at all inferior, rose from his fingertips." Plain repetition is the genre's sound: consecutive sentences may open with "It was" or "He", and flatness is never a defect to write around.

- Use the common strong verb, never the rare or poetic one: "destroyed", not "slew"; "filled", not "suffused". When the source's action is strong, let the English be strong in plain words. A stated emotion stays stated, in everyday words ("he grew nervous", "a bad feeling rose in him"); give it a body or a gesture only where the source does.
- Match the source's intensity in full, holding a charged line's charge and a plain line's plainness. Where the source is grand, be grand; where it is bare, be bare, though bareness is diction, not grammar: still supply the subject and finite verb English needs, and promote a fronted Chinese topic to a full clause rather than a participial fragment. Scale words are earned only where the source reaches for that scale.
- Let rhythm follow the source's own variation rather than manufacturing any: a stock phrase the source repeats (下一秒, 此刻) keeps one English rendering every time, a thing named twice is called the same thing twice, and synonym rotation for variety's sake is a tell, not craft. Keep "said" and "asked" as the default; reach for a marked verb only when the source marks the manner. Preserve parallelism the source builds on purpose.
- Track the source's register exactly and match it line by line: a plain or modern Chinese line becomes plain, current English, a formal line formal English, a blunt or crude line keeps its edge, and a neutral connective stays a plain connective. Keep idiom and color at exactly the source's level, so the English reaches for a turn of phrase only as far as the source itself reaches. Plain narration stays in plain modern words and plain modern syntax, never period or bookish diction or grammar the source does not have: 很快说服了自己 is "quickly convinced himself", not "talked himself round soon enough"; 想来他应该也很乐意 is "he would surely be happy about it too", not "he himself, I should think, would be more than glad of it". The same costume hides in syntax, so keep subject-verb order and current forms: "not at all inferior", never "not one whit inferior"; "even though it was a request", never "request though it was"; "didn't dare", never "dared not"; "he didn't dare touch that kind of bad luck", never "that kind of ill luck he dared not go anywhere near". Contemporary-literary constructions are costume too: the absolute phrase ("his eyes full of helplessness", never "helplessness in his eyes"), the inverted presentation ("His eyes shone with joy", never "In them was the joy"), and the pseudo-cleft ("He didn't know whether to sigh or admire it: as the screams rose...", never "What left him caught between exasperation and respect was that..."). A 的-nominalized subject unrolls into a plain frame ("When it finally took form, it was not a blade at all"), never a what-cleft ("What it finally took shape as was not a blade"). Formality lives in dialogue and address where the source puts it, not in the narrator's diction.
- Keep the lexicon everyday: the common word over the rare apt one ("a bad feeling", not "a flicker of foreboding"; "stubborn", not "obdurate"). Verbs stay strong, exact, and common; nouns and adjectives stay within the words a casual reader never notices. Connectives stay plain ("In that case", not "That being so"). Say a beat as a clause, not a packed noun phrase: "the pleasure of finally paying back an old grudge", never "the pleasure of a great grudge repaid"; "a sinister look on his face", never "a sinister cast to his face". Contractions are the default in dialogue and inner thought and welcome in narration wherever easy speech would contract ("didn't", "wasn't", "couldn't"); write the full form only for genuine emphasis or stiffly formal address.
- Re-render Chinese noun-plus-noun compounds (X海, X光, X影) that are not glossary terms as "noun of noun" or a single English noun, not new hyphenated coinages (X-sea, X-light); a glossary term keeps its given form.
- Filter verbs (saw, felt, heard) and a plain passive are ordinary English; use them freely where they keep the sentence straightforward. Then read the finished chapter once the way a reader on the site would, and rework any line that still reads as translated or as literature.

# Glossary and continuity

The glossary is memory: decisions already made. Render each glossary term with its given wording, matching the longest when two share characters. A name, title, place, technique, item, or scripture is a fixed label: when it appears it appears whole, in its given form and casing, inflected only grammatically, with the sentence recomposed freely so it reads naturally around the term. The label fixes the form, not the frequency: Chinese re-sounds a full title at every mention and English does not, so between weight-bearing uses (a scene's first mention, formal address, a status beat) refer the way English refers, with a pronoun or a plain role noun; never coin a nickname and never clip the label itself. Where an entry offers "/"-separated alternatives, use whichever fits. An idiom entry records a sense, not a script: keep its established image and render it as natural English, recasting a sentence-shaped idiom to fit the sentence it lands in. An entry that names an action (seeking the Golden Core, Comprehending the Dao) is a predicate, not a noun label: conjugate it into the sentence ("he never began seeking the Golden Core"), never wrap it into a noun phrase ("the seeking of the Golden Core that should have begun"). For a recurring term not yet in the glossary, pick one rendering on first use, keep it for the chapter, and report it among the new terms. Keep the predicate: when the source attaches an action to a term (cast, wield, shatter, comprehend), the English carries both the term and that verb, the part terse chapter titles most easily drop.

This is one installment of a long serialized novel and must read as the same book throughout: hold each character's voice, one rendering per name, and the narrator's register steady chapter to chapter; a recurring joke or set phrase keeps its first form. Honorifics carry social meaning: render the relationship the source encodes (senior and junior, master and disciple, formal and intimate) rather than flattening every form to a plain name, and preserve a first-person self-reference that encodes a humble or proud posture (collapse only the stance-neutral pronoun). # Dialogue

Tags are "said" or "asked", alone or with a plain adverb or a simple phrase ("he said calmly", "he said with a smile"); never a bare adjective ("he said, mild"). # Formatting

- Genuine system-interface status lines are bold: `**【Field: Value】**`, one per paragraph; the same words are ordinary lowercase nouns in plain prose.
- Sound effects in ALL CAPS, no quotes: BOOM, CLANG, RUMBLE.

# Grammar and punctuation

A later automatic pass enforces em-dash usage, glossary-term casing, stray 【】 brackets, and the chapter-title format, so leave those to it; still render the chapter title's meaning faithfully in natural English. Otherwise write clean English:

- No comma splices: two independent clauses take a coordinator, a period, or, rarely, a semicolon, and a Chinese 。！？ ends an English sentence, never a downgraded comma. A Chinese ，is a hinge: render it as a comma or coordinator inside one sentence, or give the clause its own sentence when it stands as a full independent statement; what it never becomes is a stranded stub. Treat the semicolon as a rare deliberate pairing, a few per chapter at most, never the everyday joint. Keep correct commas (compound sentences, introductory clauses, appositives, series). No "Although X, but Y" or "Because X, so Y" doubling; pick one connector.
- Lead with the English subject, not the topic the Chinese fronts ("He had never considered this," not "This, he had never considered"), in narration and inner thought alike.
- Default to simple past; past perfect only for genuinely earlier events, not in long had-chains; universal truths stay present. Fill in subjects the Chinese leaves implicit, naming the character wherever "he" or "his" could attach to the wrong man: a possessive whose owner is not the sentence's subject, and a run where two men trade "he" between a thought and the narration around it. Anchor a sentence-opening "this" or "that" to the noun it points back to.
- Where the source pauses or trails off with an ellipsis (…… or …), carry it across as "..." in the English: the silence is content, so leave the thought unfinished and keep the dots. Reserve a plain period or comma for sentences the source itself closes.
- Avoid AI-tell words no source produces: delve, tapestry, myriad as filler, navigate as metaphor, harness as filler. The same hard ban covers period words outside glossary terms: slew, suffused, beheld, amidst, whilst.
