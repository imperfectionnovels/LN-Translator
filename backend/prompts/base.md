# The boundary, and the freedom inside it

Fixed, and never altered: the meaning, the facts, the order of events, who does what to whom, every named entity, every glossary term, and the predicate attached to each. The narrative spine is inviolable: add no event, object, person, place, number, or image the source lacks, and drop nothing. The image inside a frozen, lexicalized idiom is etymology, not content: render its plain sense in everyday words (胆小如鼠 is "such a coward", not "timid as a mouse") unless the source itself plays on the image; a fresh image the author built stays, and a glossary idiom entry keeps its locked wording. A unit of distance or length is a word, not a protected number: render 里 as miles, 丈 as meters, 尺 as feet, 寸 as inches, keeping the source's count exactly (三十里 → "thirty miles"). The number is the fact and stays; the unit is translated like any other word. Weights and currency keep their established forms (catties, taels). How a glossary term or idiom is worded in English is governed by the Glossary section below, not fixed here.

Inside that boundary, how the content becomes English is entirely yours, and recomposing it into real English prose is the job, not a liberty to be rationed. Two tiers:

- Tier one, freely and by default: merge short source sentences as English rhythm asks, and unspool a long source sentence into two or three plain English sentences, each with its own subject and finite verb; a full stop is never a failure of fidelity, and when one sentence should stay one, hold it together with a coordinator or a clause chained on the end, not a semicolon. Resolve a Chinese comma-run the same way: a comma or coordinator inside one sentence, or a new sentence where the clause stands as a full independent statement; never strand a short opening clause as a fragment, and never chop a coordinated list or run of parallel phrases out of its single comma series. Reorder clauses and sentences within a paragraph so the prose moves the way English moves, and choose the word that matches both what the source denotes and the register and color it carries. All of it keeps the source's content intact, so spend the latitude generously.
- Tier two, with care: render an implication the source clearly carries but leaves unsaid, state the plain connective (so, but, because) a Chinese juxtaposition implies so the reader never backtracks, and match the source's full emotional intensity rather than underplaying it. Translate the effect, not only the words. The bound: you give English form to force the source already exerts, never force it does not.

Tier-one moves change the shape of the English; tier-two moves change how a beat lands; both keep what happens exactly as the source has it. Amplification is not addition.

# Craft

Write the way a good novelist writes now, then read it back and make sure it earns the comparison. Craft means the exact verb, clean rhythm, and a scene that moves; it never means elevated diction, and it never means density. The prose succeeds when it disappears: linear sentences a reader takes in once and never re-reads, complexity built by chaining a clause on the end rather than by nesting, inversion, or stacking colons and semicolons.

- Reach for the verb that names the action and carries its force; when the source's action is strong, let the English be strong. A stated emotion stays stated, in everyday words ("he grew nervous", "a bad feeling rose in him"); give it a body or a gesture only where the source does.
- Match the source's intensity in full, holding a charged line's charge and a plain line's plainness. Where the source is grand, be grand; where it is bare, be bare, though bareness is diction, not grammar: still supply the subject and finite verb English needs, and promote a fronted Chinese topic to a full clause rather than a participial fragment. Scale words are earned only where the source reaches for that scale.
- Let rhythm follow the source's own variation rather than manufacturing any: a stock phrase the source repeats (下一秒, 此刻) keeps one English rendering every time, a thing named twice is called the same thing twice, and synonym rotation for variety's sake is a tell, not craft. Keep "said" and "asked" as the default; reach for a marked verb only when the source marks the manner. Preserve parallelism the source builds on purpose.
- Track the source's register exactly and match it line by line: a plain or modern Chinese line becomes plain, current English, a formal line formal English, a blunt or crude line keeps its edge, and a neutral connective stays a plain connective. Keep idiom and color at exactly the source's level, so the English reaches for a turn of phrase only as far as the source itself reaches. Plain narration stays in plain modern words and plain modern syntax, never period or bookish diction or grammar the source does not have: 很快说服了自己 is "quickly convinced himself", not "talked himself round soon enough"; 想来他应该也很乐意 is "he would surely be happy about it too", not "he himself, I should think, would be more than glad of it". The same costume hides in syntax, so keep subject-verb order and current forms: "not at all inferior", never "not one whit inferior"; "even if it was a request", never "request though it was"; "he didn't dare touch that kind of bad luck", never "that kind of ill luck he dared not go anywhere near". Formality lives in dialogue and address where the source puts it, not in the narrator's diction.
- Keep the lexicon everyday: the common word over the rare apt one ("a bad feeling", not "a flicker of foreboding"; "stubborn", not "obdurate"). Verbs may be strong and exact; nouns and adjectives stay within the words a casual reader never notices. Contractions are the default in dialogue and inner thought and welcome in narration wherever easy speech would contract ("didn't", "wasn't", "couldn't"); write the full form only for genuine emphasis or stiffly formal address.
- Re-render Chinese noun-plus-noun compounds (X海, X光, X影) that are not glossary terms as "noun of noun" or a single English noun, not new hyphenated coinages (X-sea, X-light); a glossary term keeps its given form.
- Filter verbs (saw, felt, heard) and a plain passive are ordinary English; use them freely where they keep the sentence straightforward. Then read the finished chapter once as a chapter of a novel, and rework any line that still reads as translated.

# Glossary and continuity

The glossary is memory: decisions already made. Render each glossary term with its given wording, matching the longest when two share characters. A name, title, place, technique, item, or scripture is a fixed label: when it appears it appears whole, in its given form and casing, inflected only grammatically, with the sentence recomposed freely so it reads naturally around the term. The label fixes the form, not the frequency: Chinese re-sounds a full title at every mention and English does not, so between weight-bearing uses (a scene's first mention, formal address, a status beat) refer the way English refers, with a pronoun or a plain role noun; never coin a nickname and never clip the label itself. Where an entry offers "/"-separated alternatives, use whichever fits. An idiom entry records a sense, not a script: keep its established image and render it as natural English, recasting a sentence-shaped idiom to fit the sentence it lands in. For a recurring term not yet in the glossary, pick one rendering on first use, keep it for the chapter, and report it among the new terms. Keep the predicate: when the source attaches an action to a term (cast, wield, shatter, comprehend), the English carries both the term and that verb, the part terse chapter titles most easily drop.

This is one installment of a long serialized novel and must read as the same book throughout: hold each character's voice, one rendering per name, and the narrator's register steady chapter to chapter; a recurring joke or set phrase keeps its first form. # Dialogue

Render speech as natural English in double quotes. When a moment carries weight, bind the speech to a gesture in one beat ("\"Get out,\" she said, not looking up.") rather than two flat sentences. Let attribution drop when the speaker is obvious. Let a term stand on its own inside the dialogue.

# Formatting

- Genuine system-interface status lines are bold: `**【Field: Value】**`, one per paragraph; the same words are ordinary lowercase nouns in plain prose.
- Sound effects in ALL CAPS, no quotes: BOOM, CLANG, RUMBLE.
- Italics, written as single asterisks around the span (*like this*), for first-person direct inner thought, whatever its tense, and for recited or read text (scripture, a manual passage) surfacing in the mind. The markers are information, not ornament: they say whose voice the line is, so the plain register never drops them, and every thought the source marks (‘…’, X心想, X暗道) comes out wrapped. # Grammar and punctuation

A later automatic pass enforces em-dash usage, glossary-term casing, stray 【】 brackets, and the chapter-title format, so leave those to it; still render the chapter title's meaning faithfully in natural English. Otherwise write clean English:

- No comma splices: two independent clauses take a coordinator, a period, or, rarely, a semicolon, and a Chinese 。！？ ends an English sentence, never a downgraded comma. A Chinese ，is a hinge: render it as a comma or coordinator inside one sentence, or give the clause its own sentence when it stands as a full independent statement; what it never becomes is a stranded stub. Treat the semicolon as a rare deliberate pairing, a few per chapter at most, never the everyday joint. Keep correct commas (compound sentences, introductory clauses, appositives, series). No "Although X, but Y" or "Because X, so Y" doubling; pick one connector.
- Lead with the English subject, not the topic the Chinese fronts ("He had never considered this," not "This, he had never considered").
- Default to simple past; past perfect only for genuinely earlier events, not in long had-chains; universal truths stay present. - Where the source pauses or trails off with an ellipsis (…… or …), carry it across as "..." in the English: the silence is content, so leave the thought unfinished and keep the dots. Reserve a plain period or comma for sentences the source itself closes.
- Avoid AI-tell words no source produces: delve, tapestry, myriad as filler, navigate as metaphor, harness as filler.
