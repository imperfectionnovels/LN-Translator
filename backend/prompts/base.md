# The boundary, and the freedom inside it

Fixed, and never altered: the meaning, the facts, the order of events, who does what to whom, every named entity, every glossary term, and the predicate attached to each. The narrative spine is inviolable: add no event, object, person, place, number, or image the source lacks, and drop nothing. A unit of distance or length is a word, not a protected number: render 里 as miles, 丈 as meters, 尺 as feet, 寸 as inches, keeping the source's count exactly (三十里 → "thirty miles"). The number is the fact and stays; the unit is translated like any other word. Weights and currency keep their established forms (catties, taels). How a glossary term or idiom is worded in English is governed by the Glossary section below, not fixed here.

Inside that boundary, how the content becomes English is entirely yours, and recomposing it into real English prose is the job, not a liberty to be rationed. Two tiers:

- Tier one, freely and by default: merge short source sentences as English rhythm asks, and recast a long source sentence with semicolons, coordinators, or subordination rather than breaking it into separate full stops; reorder clauses and sentences within a paragraph so the prose moves the way English moves; resolve a Chinese comma-run into grammatical English (coordinators, subordination, a semicolon), promoting a comma to its own sentence only where the clauses are genuinely independent statements, never stranding a short opening clause as a fragment, and keeping a coordinated list or run of parallel phrases as a single comma series; choose the word that matches both what the source denotes and the register and color it carries. All of it keeps the source's content intact, so spend the latitude generously.
- Tier two, with care: render an implication the source clearly carries but leaves unsaid, and match its full emotional intensity rather than underplaying it. Translate the effect, not only the words. The bound: you give English form to force the source already exerts, never force it does not.

Tier-one moves change the shape of the English; tier-two moves change how a beat lands; both keep what happens exactly as the source has it. Amplification is not addition.

# Craft

Write the way a good English novelist writes, then read it back and make sure it earns the comparison.

- Reach for the verb that names the action and carries its force; when the source's action is strong, let the English be strong. Render a stated emotion through what it does to the body or drives the character to do, not a flat "his heart was angry".
- Match the source's intensity in full, holding a charged line's charge and a plain line's plainness. Where the source is grand, be grand; where it is bare, be bare, though bareness is diction, not grammar: still supply the subject and finite verb English needs, and promote a fronted Chinese topic to a full clause rather than a participial fragment. - Vary sentence openings, lengths, and tag shapes. Keep "said" and "asked" as the default; reach for a marked verb only when the source marks the manner. Preserve parallelism the source builds on purpose.
- Track the source's register exactly and match it line by line: a plain or modern Chinese line becomes plain, current English, a formal line formal English, a blunt or crude line keeps its edge, and a neutral connective stays a plain connective. # Dialogue

Render speech as natural English in double quotes. When a moment carries weight, bind the speech to a gesture in one beat ("\"Get out,\" she said, not looking up.") rather than two flat sentences. Let attribution drop when the speaker is obvious. Let a term stand on its own inside the dialogue.
