GENRE: science fiction. Speculative fiction grounded in technology, science, or future societies. Spans hard SF (engineering plausibility), space opera (interstellar war, galactic empires), cyberpunk (neon dystopia, hacking, megacorps), mecha (giant robots, pilots, military hierarchy), near-future thrillers, post-apocalyptic, alien-contact, time-travel. The differentiator from fantasy is that the speculative elements have a (claimed) scientific or technological basis. Even when soft, they're framed as physics, not magic.

Narrative mode: ranges by sub-genre. Hard SF leans third-person omniscient or limited with technical voice. Cyberpunk leans first-person or close third with street vernacular. Space opera leans cinematic third with multiple POVs. Match the source's choice; do not flatten. Whatever the mode, POV still changes only at paragraph or scene breaks per base.md, never mid-paragraph or mid-sentence: omniscient breadth means ranging across scenes, not head-hopping inside one.

Target prose: precise. Technical vocabulary is a feature, not a bug; jargon is allowed when the source uses it, and stays consistent across the novel.

Voice register:
- Narrator: register matches sub-genre. Hard SF narrator is dry and exacting; cyberpunk narrator is clipped and noir; space opera narrator is sweeping and elevated.
- Dialogue: characters speak from their training. Engineers talk like engineers (units, tolerances, failure modes). Soldiers talk like soldiers (chain-of-command, callsigns, ROE). Hackers talk like hackers (handles, exploits, opsec). Don't homogenize.
- Interior thought: technical when characters think technically. A pilot under fire isn't musing on the beauty of stars; they're tracking the bandit at their seven, checking fuel state, weighing missile inventory.

Speculative vocabulary, preserved consistently:
- FTL drive names (warp, hyperspace, jump, slipstream, fold), ship classes (frigate, destroyer, battlecruiser), weapon types (railgun, plasma cannon, mass driver), AI types (general AI, narrow AI, ASI), augmentations (cyberware, wetware, neural lace, exocortex), corporations (megacorp, zaibatsu, kombinat).
- Glossary discipline is essential: a "psyker" stays a psyker for the whole novel, not "esper" in chapter 1 and "telepath" in chapter 12.
- Cyberpunk slang (chrome, ice, jack-in, sprawl, console cowboy, ripperdoc): preserve as in-genre vocabulary; do not "modernize" to plain words.

Units and measurements:
- Metric stays metric; imperial stays imperial; SI prefixes preserved. "12 GW," "8 light-years," "0.8 c," "3 AU."
- Numerals stay numerals when technical. "Throttle to 80%" not "throttle to eighty percent."
- Astronomical scale: "10⁹ km" preferred over "one billion km" when source uses scientific notation.

Dialogue protocol: callsigns, radio chatter, military exchange:
- Preserve callsigns as proper nouns: "Viper Lead," "Bravo Six," "Cortez Actual."
- Radio brevity codes (Roger, Wilco, Affirmative, Negative, Out, Over): render in the standard radio register; do not translate to conversational equivalents.
- Military rank: render with the source's hierarchy. "Lieutenant Commander Kim" not "Commander Kim" if rank is full-spelled.
- ALL-CAPS for klaxon / system text: "HULL BREACH DETECTED" stays ALL-CAPS; an automated AI voice in dialogue may stay ALL-CAPS or render in italics per glossary (the Markdown output has no small-caps, so do not attempt it).

Cyberpunk register (apply when sub-genre is cyberpunk):
- Sentence rhythm shorter, more fragments. "Streets wet. Neon bleeding. Couldn't see the rain for the glare."
- Brand-name / megacorp references as proper nouns. Future-trademark vocabulary intact.
- First-person voice often laconic, profane in moderation, knowing.

Space-opera register (apply when sub-genre is space opera):
- Cinematic. Wide shots described before close shots. Fleet engagements rendered with their tactical geometry intact (capital ship line, fighter screen, flanking maneuver).
- Honorifics and titles for nobility / military: render fully ("Admiral the Right Honourable Lord Telmar of House Kestrel") if source uses them; truncating loses register.

Hard SF register (apply when source leans hard):
- Treat speculative concepts as physics. If the source posits a Krasnikov tube, render the explanation faithfully; do not handwave to "they went really fast."
- Math / equations preserved. "E = γmc²" stays "E = γmc²."

Watch-list:
- Do NOT translate technical jargon into plain English. "Quantum entanglement comms" is not "magic instant talking."
- Do NOT modernize cyberpunk slang. "Jack into the deck" is not "log onto the computer."
- Do NOT flatten military precision. "Engaging target at bearing 240, range 12 klicks" is not "shooting at the thing that's far away."
- Do NOT spell out unit abbreviations. "GW" stays "GW," "c" stays "c," "AU" stays "AU."
- Do NOT translate AI dialogue into emotional register if the source treats the AI as cold. Match the source's framing of machine vs human voice.
