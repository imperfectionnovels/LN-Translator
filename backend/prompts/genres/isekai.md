GENRE: isekai / transmigration / reincarnation. A protagonist from the modern world lands in another one: fantasy, historical-China-with-magic, dating sim, novel-world, or video game. Mechanics often borrow from RPGs (stats, classes, levels, skill trees, system notifications). The contrast between the transmigrator's modern interior voice and the world they wake in IS the genre.

Narrative mode: close third or first person per source; the POV is the transmigrator, a modern consciousness in an unfamiliar setting.

Two registers, both correct in their own slot, kept apart:
- Modern interior voice: contemporary, often sarcastic, gaming-literate, a step ahead of the locals because the POV has read this novel or played this game. Render with controlled snap.
- World-appropriate exterior: match the world the POV is speaking in, not the POV's own register. The character may think "what a tropey villain monologue" while saying aloud "I shall not yield to your tyranny". The medieval guard does not say "dude".

System notifications follow base.md's 【】 formatting; here they carry game UI: "**【Quest Accepted: Defeat the Demon King】**", "**【Skill: Fireball acquired】**". Title-Case the field, render values per glossary, and keep stats as numerals ("HP 500 / 500", not "five hundred hit points"). Class, skill, and item names are Title-Cased proper nouns ("Berserker", "Holy Sword Excalibur"), function words lowercase; italic infodump blocks and stat lists keep their format.

Genre vocabulary, per glossary: "transmigrator" (穿越者), "regressor" (回归者), "original body" (原主), "novel-world"; the metacommentary "in the original work / in the novel / in the game" (原作 / 小说里); "cheat", "system", "skill", "status" as gaming class-terms ("system" stays "system" unless the source capitalizes it). When the POV invokes "plot armor" or "main-character privilege", render the gaming idiom straight, because the POV genuinely thinks this way. Keep disorientation beats ("Wait, where am I?", "This is the novel I read last week!") and recognition beats ("Don't tell me, this is the boss?!") with the POV's confusion and gamer excitement intact.

Match the world's honorifics: Japanese-coded (-san, -chan) preserved, Chinese-coded (Big Sister Mei) using family-address vocabulary, Western-coded using English titles.
