GENRE: slice-of-life. Small-scale, low-stakes, everyday fiction. Cafe stories, cooking, healing, friendship, found family, retirement, a quiet village life. Sometimes paired with light fantasy (a cozy adventurer's retirement, a witch running a tea shop), sometimes purely contemporary. Plot is incidental; mood, sensory detail, and small character beats are the point.

Narrative mode: usually close third-person, past tense. POV often stays with one character whose interior life maps to the small world around them.

Target prose: warm, observational, attentive to small detail. The reader should be able to taste the food, smell the rain, hear the kettle. Restrained, sentimental but not saccharine. Slow rhythms; let beats land.

Voice register:
- Narrator: gentle, observational, attentive. The voice trusts small details to carry meaning without telegraphing the emotion they should evoke. The reader notices the half-empty teacup; the narrator does not announce that it represents loneliness.
- External dialogue: natural conversation, often unhurried. Pauses, "..." (trailing off), and silence carry weight. Render the source's pacing. If a character takes three lines to answer a question, preserve the delay.
- Internal thought: intimate but quiet. The POV's interior is not dramatically tortured; it is honestly observed.

Pacing:
- Slow it down. Slice-of-life prose lives in the moment-to-moment: the steam rising from a cup, the cat stretching on a windowsill, the way light falls through a paper screen. When the source dwells, the translation must dwell.
- Do NOT compress sensory detail into efficient prose. The detail is the point. If the source spends a paragraph describing the texture of dough being kneaded, render that paragraph in full; don't summarize to "she kneaded the dough."
- Short sentences are fine. Fragments are fine when the source uses them. Slice-of-life prose often has a haiku-adjacent compression.

Sensory detail:
- Food: cuisine vocabulary matters. Preserve dish names and ingredient names per glossary. If the source says "an onigiri filled with umeboshi," render that. Don't domesticate to "a rice ball with pickled plum" unless the glossary has chosen the domesticated form.
- Weather and season: seasonal markers are often central to slice-of-life mood. First snow, plum-blossom rain, the particular blue of an autumn sky. Render the source's seasonal vocabulary precisely.
- Domestic sounds: a kettle, footsteps on tatami, a screen door sliding. These are the genre's atmospheric currency.

Dialogue conventions:
- Children's speech: render the imperfect grammar, mispronunciations, and direct emotional honesty children bring. Don't smooth a child's "I want to go too!" into something adult.
- Elderly characters: render the particular speech patterns of older characters: slightly more formal, occasional period vocabulary, slower rhythm. Don't update their speech to contemporary norms.
- Animals: characters often talk to animals. The animal does not respond verbally, but the dialogue beat lands as a quiet character moment. Preserve.

Tone:
- Avoid melodrama. Slice-of-life is the genre of restrained emotion. A character finds out a beloved cat has died: the source rarely describes hours of weeping; it describes the character making tea, hands shaking slightly, then drinking it cold. Render that restraint.
- Comedy in slice-of-life is observational and gentle, not jokes-per-page. A wry aside from the narrator, a child mispronouncing a word, a misunderstanding between neighbors. Preserve the source's specific comic register.

Watch-list:
- Avoid the temptation to make plot happen. If a chapter is "the protagonist tends the garden and thinks about the season," render exactly that. Don't add stakes the source did not include.
- Avoid AI-pulp prose tics: "her heart felt warm," "a sense of peace washed over her," "she couldn't help but smile." Slice-of-life is harmed by these stock-sentimental beats more than other genres because they crowd out the specific.
- Do not over-tag dialogue. In quiet conversation, attribution can drop frequently; let the reader follow the speakers from context.
- Preserve incidental dialogue (greetings, small talk, ordering food at a counter). The mundane texture is the genre.
