GENRE: slice-of-life. Small-scale, low-stakes everyday fiction: cafes, cooking, healing, friendship, found family, a quiet village, sometimes with light fantasy (a witch running a tea shop). Plot is incidental; mood, sensory detail, and small character beats are the point.

Narrative mode: usually close third person, past tense, staying with one character whose interior life maps to the small world around them.

Target prose: warm, observational, attentive to small detail; the reader should taste the food, smell the rain, hear the kettle. Sentimental but never saccharine, with slow rhythms that let beats land. The narrator trusts a small detail to carry meaning without announcing it: the reader notices the half-empty teacup, and the narrator does not say it means loneliness. Inner thought is quiet and honestly observed, not dramatically tortured.

Pacing is the craft: slow it down. When the source dwells on the steam off a cup or dough being kneaded, render that in full; do not summarize sensory detail into efficient prose, because the detail is the point. Short sentences and fragments are fine where the source uses them.

Sensory specifics carry the mood: keep dish and ingredient names per glossary ("an onigiri filled with umeboshi", not domesticated unless the glossary chose that form), render seasonal markers precisely (first snow, plum-blossom rain, the particular blue of an autumn sky), and keep domestic sounds (a kettle, footsteps on tatami, a sliding screen door).

Dialogue: render a child's imperfect, direct speech without smoothing it into adult phrasing; keep an elderly character's slower, slightly formal rhythm; preserve a one-sided conversation with an animal as the quiet character beat it is; keep incidental small talk (greetings, ordering at a counter), because the mundane texture is the genre. Attribution can drop often in quiet conversation.

Tone is restrained: where a beloved cat dies, the source tends to show the character making tea with shaking hands and drinking it cold rather than weeping for hours, so render that restraint. Comedy is observational and gentle (a wry aside, a child's mispronunciation, a neighborly misunderstanding), not jokes-per-page.

Watch for: inventing stakes the source did not include; and the stock-sentimental tics that crowd out the specific ("her heart felt warm", "a sense of peace washed over her", "she couldn't help but smile").
