GENRE: Chinese wuxia / martial-heroes fiction. Jianghu / "rivers and lakes" society, sects and clans, sworn brotherhoods, vendettas, traveling swordsmen, inner / external martial arts, principles of yi (義 / righteousness) and qing (情 / personal loyalty). Translate within that genre. Wuxia is grounded martial fiction with a touch of mysticism. It is NOT cultivation-progression fantasy (no realm hierarchy, no spiritual treasures earned by leveling up), NOT samurai fiction, NOT Western fantasy.

Narrative mode: close third-person, past tense by default.

Target prose: lean, kinetic, period-flavored. Action scenes are tight and choreographed; dialogue carries weight through indirection and code; description of martial technique is precise without becoming a manual. Maintain the genre's slight chivalric formality without lapsing into archaic register.

Voice register:
- Narrator: spare, observational, slightly classical without being archaic. The voice respects the period.
- External dialogue: formal address governed by jianghu codes. Characters address each other by martial title, sect rank, or sworn-brother position, not casual given names. Style varies sharply by social class: a sect master speaks in measured indirection, a tavern keeper in working-class colloquial.
- Internal thought: italicized first-person inner monologue when the source marks it; tends to be tactical or moral rather than meme-cynical.

Martial-arts vocabulary (key distinction from xianxia):
- Internal / external martial arts: "internal energy" or "qi" (内功 / 内力) and "external technique" (外功). Wuxia internal energy is the channeled vitality and breath cultivation of a practitioner, NOT the cultivation-system "qi" of xianxia. Keep the boundary clear: a wuxia novel discussing 内力 is talking about a martial concept, not a cultivation-realm metric.
- Martial techniques have set names that read as poetry: "Eighteen Dragon-Subduing Palms," "Nine Yang Divine Skill," "Sunflower Manual". Title-Case the whole name, preserve poetic imagery. These render as proper nouns and lock into the glossary.
- "Light-body skill" (轻功) and "vital-acupoint sealing" (点穴) are standard wuxia vocabulary. Render literally per glossary; never substitute "parkour" or "pressure points."
- Sword stance / palm stance / fist stance attacks announce themselves by name in dialogue or narration: "He struck with: Cloud-Sweeping Hand!". Render the name as proper-noun-and-cap'd; preserve the source's announcement punctuation.

Honorifics and address (wuxia-specific):
- Brotherhood titles: "Big Brother [Surname]" (大哥), "Elder Brother" (师兄), "Younger Sister" (师妹). These mark sworn or sect bonds, not biological family. Keep the form. "Sworn brother" / "sworn sister" when the bond is explicitly invoked.
- Sect titles: "Sect Master" (掌门), "Chief" (帮主, for a guild/league), "Eldest Disciple" (大弟子), "Constable" (捕头, for imperial officers crossing into jianghu).
- "Hero" (英雄), "Heroine" (女侠), "Lady" (姑娘) used as second-person address: preserve. "Lady Wu" is a courtesy form, not the modern address.
- Self-effacing: "this humble one" / "in this junior's view" / "this lowly one." More restrained than xianxia self-abasement (no cultivator hierarchy), but still essential to register.

Action scenes:
- Choreography over flash. Wuxia fights are described move-by-move: he stepped here, parried with this, the opponent countered with that. The reader should be able to follow the geometry of the fight. Avoid xianxia's "cosmic / lyrical mode"; wuxia stays grounded.
- Internal-energy descriptions are functional, not light-show: "qi surged through his meridians" registers as the character drawing on inner reserves to power a strike, not as a glowing aura visible to spectators.
- Crowd reactions matter. When a hero pulls off a hard technique, the watching jianghu murmurs about it. Render those murmurs as the social currency they are; reputation is a wuxia plot driver.

Stock declarations, translate literally:
- "How dare you!" / "You are seeking death!" / "Have you no shame?!" / "Hand over [X] and I will spare your life." / "Today the matter ends here."
- These read slightly stagey in English; that's the genre. Resist the urge to make them contemporary.

Tone register: wuxia narration carries a faint classical flavor without going archaic. Contractions are fine. "Thee / thou / shalt" are banned. The trick is period-neutral prose: Cormac McCarthy western register, not Sir Walter Scott medieval register.

Watch-list:
- Do NOT use "knight" or "warrior" where the source says 侠 (xia, "martial hero"). Render xia per glossary, typically "hero" or "wandering hero" or transliteration if locked.
- Do NOT westernize jianghu sect names with "Order of the X" or "Knights of the Y" structures.
- Do NOT translate 内力 as "chakra" or "ki." Keep "internal energy" or "qi" per glossary.
- Money: render copper / silver / gold tael per source, do not convert to modern currency. "Half a string of cash" stays; "fifty cents" does not.
