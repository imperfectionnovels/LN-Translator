GENRE: Chinese wuxia / martial-heroes fiction. Jianghu ("rivers and lakes") society, sects and clans, sworn brotherhoods, vendettas, wandering swordsmen, internal and external martial arts, the codes of yi (義, righteousness) and qing (情, loyalty). Grounded martial fiction with a touch of mysticism: no cultivation realms, no leveling-up treasures. Not xianxia, not samurai fiction, not Western fantasy.

Narrative mode: close third person, past tense by default.

Target prose: lean, kinetic, period-flavored. Tight, choreographed action; dialogue that carries weight through indirection and code; martial technique described precisely without turning into a manual. Aim for period-neutral prose with a faint classical flavor and no archaism (Cormac McCarthy western register, not Walter Scott medieval); contractions are fine, "thee/thou/shalt" are banned.

Voice: a spare, observational narrator that respects the period; dialogue governed by jianghu codes, where rank and bond decide the form of address and a sect master's measured indirection differs sharply from a tavern keeper's working-class speech; italic first-person inner thought that runs tactical or moral rather than meme-cynical.

Martial vocabulary (the key line against xianxia): "internal energy" / "qi" (内功 / 内力) and "external technique" (外功) are martial concepts, the channeled vitality and breath of a practitioner, not a cultivation-realm metric, and qi surging through the meridians powers a strike rather than lighting up an aura spectators can see. Named techniques read as poetry and lock as proper nouns ("Eighteen Dragon-Subduing Palms", "Nine Yang Divine Skill"). "Light-body skill" (轻功) and "vital-acupoint sealing" (点穴) render literally, never "parkour" or "pressure points". A named stance announced in combat keeps its name and the source's punctuation ("He struck: Cloud-Sweeping Hand!").

Honorifics and address: brotherhood titles ("Big Brother" 大哥, "Elder Brother" 师兄, "Younger Sister" 师妹) mark sworn or sect bonds, not blood, with "sworn brother / sister" when the bond is explicitly invoked; sect titles ("Sect Master" 掌门, "Chief" 帮主, "Eldest Disciple" 大弟子, "Constable" 捕头); "Hero" (英雄), "Heroine" (女侠), "Lady" (姑娘) as courtesy address; self-effacing "this humble one" / "this junior", more restrained than xianxia self-abasement.

Action: choreography over flash. The reader follows the geometry of the fight move by move (stepped here, parried with this, countered with that). Stay grounded, with no xianxia cosmic mode. Crowd reactions are social currency: when a hero pulls off a hard technique the watching jianghu murmurs, and reputation drives the plot.

Stock declarations stay literal and slightly stagey ("How dare you!", "You are seeking death!", "Hand over [X] and I will spare your life", "Today the matter ends here").

Watch for: render 侠 as "hero" / "wandering hero" per glossary, not "knight" or "warrior"; keep jianghu sect names as themselves, not "Order of the X" or "Knights of the Y"; keep 内力 as "internal energy" / "qi", not "chakra" or "ki"; keep period money (copper / silver / gold tael, "half a string of cash"), never modern currency.
