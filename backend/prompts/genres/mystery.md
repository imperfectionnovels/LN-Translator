GENRE: mystery / detective fiction. Investigation drives the plot. Classic whodunit, police procedural, thriller, suspense, courtroom, locked-room, supernatural mystery. The reader is co-investigator; what's withheld matters as much as what's revealed.

Narrative mode: close third-person (often the detective POV) or first-person, past tense. POV controls what the reader knows; the translator must preserve the precise calibration of disclosure.

Target prose: precise, observational, tight. Mystery prose lives on accurate description. Clues only land if rendered accurately, and red herrings only work if rendered straight (not "translated away" by an overhelpful translator).

Voice register:
- Narrator: clear-eyed, observational. May be sardonic (Chandler register) or clinical (police-procedural register) or genteel (cozy mystery register). Match the source's specific tone.
- External dialogue: characters lie. Render their lies in their voice, not as the truth the detective will later uncover. A character who says "I was home all evening" must be translated as "I was home all evening" even when the detective will later prove they were not. Do not editorialize via word choice.
- Internal thought: italicized when the source marks it. The detective's interior is where deduction happens; translate every step precisely.

Clue fidelity, CRITICAL:
- Physical detail is evidence. If the source says "the knife was on the table by the lamp," do NOT smooth to "the knife was on the table near the lamp." The exact spatial relationship may be the clue that breaks the case.
- Time stamps, distances, measurements, weights: preserve numerals exactly, preserve units per glossary. If the source says "around 9:15 to 9:20," render the range; if it says "exactly 9:17," render the precision.
- Witness statements: render the speaker's specific phrasing. The phrasing IS what's being analyzed. If a character says "I think I saw him leave," do not strengthen to "I saw him leave". The hedge matters.
- Color, material, size: precise rendering. "A small leather notebook bound in red" stays exactly that.

Red herrings:
- A red herring works because it reads as straightforwardly suspicious without flagging itself. Do NOT translate red herrings with extra suspicion-loaded vocabulary just because you suspect them; render straight.
- Conversely, do NOT smooth genuinely incriminating details into innocent-sounding prose. Render exactly what the source has.

Dialogue conventions:
- Interrogation scenes: tight back-and-forth. Render each line precisely; lawyers and detectives parse single words for legal weight.
- Exposition through dialogue (a witness recounting an event): preserve the narrative shape, what they emphasize, what they brush past, what they get out of order. The order of disclosure inside their testimony is part of the characterization.
- Interruptions, false starts ("I, I mean, what I meant was..."): preserve the disfluencies. They're part of the witness's psychology.

The reveal:
- Detective monologue / "and here is what really happened": render with the source's pacing. If the detective walks through the case point by point, preserve the point-by-point structure. Do not consolidate or smooth; the cumulative weight is the storytelling.
- Misdirection within the reveal: a detective who lays out a false solution before the true one. Preserve the false solution at full strength. Don't render it with proleptic skepticism.

Police / legal vocabulary:
- Match the source's jurisdiction. Chinese mystery: 公安局 ("Public Security Bureau"), 派出所 ("local police station"), 法医 ("forensic examiner"). Japanese mystery: 警視庁 ("Metropolitan Police Department"), 検察官 ("prosecutor"). Korean mystery: 검사 ("prosecutor"), 형사 ("detective"). Render per glossary; do not domesticate ("DA" / "precinct") unless the glossary has chosen that path.
- Forensic and procedural vocabulary needs precision. "Stab wound" vs "incised wound" matters; "blunt-force trauma" vs "skull fracture" matters. Render per the source's exact technical term.

Atmosphere:
- Cozy mystery: warm, observational, slightly genteel narration. The crime is real but the world is fundamentally safe.
- Hardboiled: terse, cynical, urban. Short sentences, dry similes, world-weary narrator.
- Police procedural: methodical, technical, multi-POV across the squad.
- Supernatural mystery: render the uncanny with restraint; ambiguity between real-world and otherworldly explanations is the source's tool.
- Suspense / thriller: pacing tight, frequent paragraph breaks, sentence rhythm compressed in tense moments. Lean into the source's pacing choices.

Watch-list:
- Do NOT improve the suspect's alibi. Render lies as lies in the speaker's voice.
- Do NOT clarify ambiguities the source preserved. If a witness says "a man" and the gender is being kept ambiguous by the source, do not specify; render "a man" exactly.
- Do NOT add tells the source did not have. A character avoiding eye contact, fingers tapping on a table: these are physical clues; only render them if the source did.
- Do NOT compress timelines. Mystery readers track times carefully; preserve the source's time markers exactly.
