GENRE: Chinese xianxia / cultivation-progression fantasy. Sect politics, realm hierarchy, spirit-treasures, reincarnation, righteous-versus-demonic conflict, dry web-novel humor. Write it as xianxia, not as Western epic fantasy, YA fantasy, or LitRPG.

Narrative mode: close third person, past tense by default.

Target prose: clean, taut, atmospheric. Direct where the source is direct, ceremonious in titles and ranks, dry in the narrator's humor, the same novel chapter to chapter. Read xianxia as literature: choose forceful, idiomatic verbs and let scenes carry their own weight. Faithful is not the same as timid.

Three voices, kept distinct:
- Narrator: dry, sardonic, lightly worldly, aphoristic when the source goes there.
- Dialogue: xianxia-formal, honorific-heavy disciple-and-elder speech; formality comes from honorifics and indirection, not from "thee/thou" (contractions are fine).
- Inner thought (first person only): when the raw breaks to a first-person pronoun (我 / 吾 / 在下) with no tag, render it italic in the character's voice, and let it carry modern-cynic or meme register when the raw thought does (新手村 to "newbie village", 溜了溜了 to "I'm out, I'm out"). Strategic reasoning the raw keeps in the third person is free indirect discourse: leave it roman and third person, no slang. Italics never changes grammatical person.

Scene modes (switch at paragraph breaks; the contrast is the pacing cue):
- Action: short percussive beats, single-verb sentences, frequent breaks; do not stack subordinate clauses to imitate Chinese parataxis. Here the short-beat rhythm overrides the general rule about joining comma-runs into longer flowing sentences.
- Conversational: medium-length sentences, tags interleaved with body language.
- Cosmic / lyrical (high-realm set-pieces, void-crossing, weighted closings): longer flowing sentences, parallel structure allowed, and the elevated scale words concentrated here rather than sprinkled across the chapter (a scale word the source itself reaches for outside this mode still renders).

Cultivator titles: a personal name plus 真人 / 真君 / 上人 / 道人 renders title-first ("True Person [Name]"); a descriptive epithet plus the same suffix renders epithet-first ("Demon-Purging True Person"). One order per name.

Forms of address carry status; render them per source. Only stance-neutral first person collapses: plain 我 as "I", 咱 / 咱们 as "I" / "we" (carry any rustic register in the surrounding diction, not the pronoun). Every self-reference that encodes a posture, humble or arrogant, is kept, because the posture is what English cannot recover later and what makes a deliberate shift (humble to neutral to arrogant) legible.
- Humble / deferential self-reference, kept: 在下 "this humble one", 弟子 "this disciple", 贫道 "this humble Daoist", 贫僧 "this humble monk", 属下 "this subordinate", 老夫 "this old man", 妾身 "this humble woman" (only "this concubine" when she actually is one).
- Elevated / arrogant self-reference, equally kept (the opposite pole, so it is not swallowed by "I" either): 本座 "This Seat", 本尊 "This Venerable One", 本王 "This King", 本宫 "This Palace", 本帝 "This Emperor", 朕 "We" (imperial, emperor only).
- Establish the stance, then let English breathe: use the marked form on the weight-bearing references (introductions, formal address, deference or status beats, the first reference of a speech or scene), then pronominalize immediate repeats to "I" within that same speech once it is established. A later drop to plain 我 then reads as defiance or a claim of equality, not as the form vanishing.
- 弟子 and 属下 are also ordinary nouns ("the disciples", "his subordinates"): use the self-reference rendering only when the word is the speaker's own first-person reference, not on the bare string.
- Titled second person: Senior Brother / Junior Sister / Martial Uncle / Master / True Monarch / Dao Lord / Fellow Daoist / Ancestor. Inferior to superior uses the title or title plus name; peers use "Fellow Daoist" plus surname; a contemptuous superior uses a bare name or "boy / brat". Preserve mid-scene address upgrades (guest to senior to supplication); the shift telegraphs a recalibration of rank or threat.
- Honorific gestures keep the hand position ("clasped hands and bowed", "cupped his hands", "kowtowed"); do not flatten to "bowed". Buddhist-Daoist vocabulary (Amitabha, Dharma, karma, Avici) renders literally per glossary.

Realm names take their established English forms, never transliterated: Foundation Establishment, Golden Core, Nascent Soul, Divine Transformation, Tribulation Transcendence, Ascension (not Jindan, Yuanying, Du Jie). A realm or stage modifier attaches to a cultivator, not to a clan or sect unless the source classes the organization by tier ("a small clan whose strongest was only early Foundation Establishment", not "early Foundation Establishment clan").

Casing: a role label takes Title Case when it stands in as the name of one specific character right now (Senior Sister Wang, Master, the Hall Master), and stays lowercase when generic, plural, or a class (the inner disciples, fellow daoists, the elders). Genuine proper nouns (sects, places, named techniques and treasures, scripture titles, ranks used as titles) keep Title Case; generic metaphysical vocabulary (qi, spiritual power, dao, karma, sea of consciousness) stays sentence-case even when it recurs. A pronoun for a supreme being (Dao Lord, World-Honored One, Buddha) is reverently capitalized (He / His). The automatic pass handles Heavenly-Stem and Earthly-Branch elemental compounds, so do not fuss over their casing.

Genre signatures, rendered faithfully and never invented:
- Stock declarations stay literal and a touch stagey ("Are you looking to die?!", "How dare you!", "Senior, spare me!"); the slight theatricality is the register, so resist making them contemporary.
- The time-compression aside ("all this takes a while to tell but happened in an instant"; "the time an incense stick takes to burn") is a genre move: render it where the source has it, at most once or twice per action chapter.
- Pull a reaction beat (a face paling, an onlooker gasping) onto its own line. A compact aphorism at an emotional peak keeps its parallel structure and present tense; do not expand it into prose.
- Locked idioms keep their established wording but may inflect for grammar (找死 as "You court death" standalone, "courting death" inside a phrase).
- 明明 …（此刻）却 … marks a present, simultaneous contrast (X plainly still holds, yet now Y), not a past state: "the heat still rolled around them, yet now it was cold as winter", never "a moment before it had been hot".

Watch for: literal calques (话音刚落 to "as he finished speaking", not "as his words fell"); narrator-voice "How could he?!" (reserve it for dialogue or inner thought); time-anchor overuse ("At this moment", "the next second"), near twice per scene at most; and arbitrary modern filler in classical prose ("kinda", "his brain shut down", "epic" plus a combat noun) unless the inner monologue's register is deliberately modern.
