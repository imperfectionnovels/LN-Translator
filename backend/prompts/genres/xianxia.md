GENRE: Chinese xianxia / cultivation-progression fantasy: sect politics, realm hierarchy, spirit-treasures, reincarnation, righteous-versus-demonic conflict, dry web-novel humor. Xianxia, not Western epic fantasy, YA, or LitRPG. The world is ancient but the prose is not: formality lives in honorifics and indirection, never "thee/thou", never avoided contractions (don't / can't belong in cultivator speech; full forms for oaths).

Three voices, kept distinct:
- Narrator: dry, sardonic, aphoristic when the source is.
- Dialogue: xianxia-formal, honorific-heavy.
- Inner thought: the viewpoint character's thought renders as first-person present-tense italic direct thought even where the raw frames it in third person or past (在X看来, X心想, X暗道); carry his modern-cynic or meme register where the raw has it (新手村 "newbie village"). Other characters and event-narration stay third person. If the novel's brief assigns a different thought format, the brief wins.

Scene modes (switch at paragraph breaks):
- Action: short percussive beats, single-verb sentences, frequent breaks. Covers sequential physical action only; never chops a list or dialogue into fragments.
- Conversational: medium sentences, tags interleaved with body language.
- Cosmic / lyrical (high-realm set-pieces, weighted closings): longer flowing sentences, parallel structure allowed, scale words concentrated here.

Titles and address:
- A personal name plus 真人 / 真君 / 上人 / 道人 renders title-first ("True Person [Name]"); a descriptive epithet renders epithet-first ("Demon-Purging True Person"); one order per name. A rank used as a title renders rank-first ("Heavenly Immortal [Name]"), never "[Name] the Heavenly Immortal". Full-title frequency follows the base rule: weight-bearing uses whole, he / him / the True Person between.
- Only the stance-neutral first person collapses to "I" / "we". A posture-encoding self-reference is kept: establish it on weight-bearing references (introductions, formal address, status beats), then pronominalize to "I"; a later drop to plain 我 reads as defiance. Humble: 在下 "this humble one", 弟子 "this disciple", 贫道 "this humble Daoist", 贫僧 "this humble monk", 属下 "this subordinate", 老夫 "this old man", 妾身 "this humble woman". Elevated: 本座 "This Seat", 本尊 "This Venerable One", 本王 "This King", 本宫 "This Palace", 本帝 "This Emperor", 朕 "We". 弟子 / 属下 are also ordinary nouns ("the disciples"); the marked rendering applies only to the speaker's own first person.
- Titled second person (Senior Brother, Junior Sister, Martial Uncle, Master, Fellow Daoist, Ancestor) renders per source; preserve mid-scene address upgrades. Honorific gestures keep the hand position ("clasped hands and bowed", "kowtowed"). Buddhist-Daoist vocabulary (Amitabha, Dharma, karma) renders literally per glossary.

Realms and coinage:
- Realm names take their established English, never transliterated: Foundation Establishment, Golden Core, Nascent Soul, Divine Transformation, Tribulation Transcendence, Ascension. A realm or stage attaches to a cultivator, not an organization, unless the source classes it by tier. Stage modifiers hyphenate: early-stage, mid-stage, late-stage, peak.
- Coining a NEW technique, art, or treasure name: a verb-object element becomes an Object-Verbing hyphenated attributive, no commas, every element kept including the suffix (破邪显正剑诀: "Evil-Breaking Righteousness-Revealing Sword Art"), never a gerund chain. Verb-object only; the base noun-noun rule stands.

Casing: a role label is Title Case when it names one specific character right now (Senior Sister Wang, the Hall Master), lowercase when generic or plural (the inner disciples). Proper nouns keep Title Case; generic metaphysical vocabulary (qi, spiritual power, dao, karma) stays sentence-case. A pronoun for a supreme being is reverently capitalized (He / His).

Genre signatures, never invented: stock declarations stay literal and a touch stagey ("Are you looking to die?!") and keep their ！; narrator-voice "How could he?!" stays in dialogue or thought; the incense-stick time aside renders where the source has it; a reaction beat takes its own line; an aphorism keeps its parallel structure and present tense; locked idioms keep their established wording and may inflect (找死: "You court death" / "courting death"); 明明…却 marks a present, simultaneous contrast. Calque watch: 话音刚落 is "as he finished speaking", never "as his words fell"; no time anchor the source does not have; no modern filler unless the inner voice is deliberately modern.
