GENRE: Chinese xianxia / cultivation-progression fantasy. Sect politics, realm hierarchy, spirit-treasures, reincarnation, righteous-versus-demonic conflict, dry web-novel humor. Write it as xianxia, not Western epic fantasy, YA fantasy, or LitRPG.

Target prose: clean, clear, fast-moving, ceremonious where titles and ranks are spoken, dry in the narrator's humor, steady chapter to chapter. Read xianxia the way its best published translations read: plain modern narration that contracts the way those translations do (didn't, wasn't, couldn't), strong common verbs, scenes that carry their own weight; the world is ancient but the prose is not. Faithful is not timid.

Three voices, kept distinct: narrator (dry, sardonic, aphoristic when the source is); dialogue (xianxia-formal, honorific-heavy; formality comes from honorifics and indirection, not "thee/thou" and not from avoiding contractions: don't / can't / that's belong in cultivator speech too, with full forms kept for oaths and formal proclamations); inner thought, the viewpoint character's own, rendered as first-person present-tense italic direct thought even where the raw frames it in third person or the past (在X看来, X心想, X暗道), which overrides the base free-indirect default. Carry his modern-cynic or meme register where the raw has it (新手村 "newbie village", 溜了溜了 "I'm out"). Other characters and plain event-narration stay third person.

Scene modes (switch at paragraph breaks; the contrast is the pacing cue):
- Action: short percussive beats, single-verb sentences, frequent breaks; do not stack clauses to imitate Chinese parataxis. This short-beat license covers a run of sequential physical action only; it never chops a coordinated list, enumeration, or line of dialogue into fragments, which keeps its commas.
- Conversational: medium-length sentences, tags interleaved with body language.
- Cosmic / lyrical (high-realm set-pieces, void-crossing, weighted closings): longer flowing sentences, parallel structure allowed, the elevated scale words concentrated here rather than sprinkled (a scale word the source reaches for elsewhere still renders).

Cultivator titles: a personal name plus 真人 / 真君 / 上人 / 道人 renders title-first ("True Person [Name]"); a descriptive epithet plus the same suffix renders epithet-first ("Demon-Purging True Person"). One order per name. A rank or realm used as a title before a personal name renders rank-first ("Heavenly Immortal [Name]"), never "[Name] the Heavenly Immortal". The raw re-sounds the full title at every mention; the English does not: give the title its weight-bearing uses whole (scene entrance, formal address, a status beat) and refer with he / him / the True Person between them.

Forms of address carry status; render them per source. Only stance-neutral first person collapses (我 "I"; 咱 / 咱们 "I" / "we", with any rustic register in the surrounding diction, not the pronoun). Keep every self-reference that encodes a posture, since the posture is what English cannot recover later:
- Humble: 在下 "this humble one", 弟子 "this disciple", 贫道 "this humble Daoist", 贫僧 "this humble monk", 属下 "this subordinate", 老夫 "this old man", 妾身 "this humble woman".
- Elevated: 本座 "This Seat", 本尊 "This Venerable One", 本王 "This King", 本宫 "This Palace", 本帝 "This Emperor", 朕 "We" (emperor only).
Establish the marked form on weight-bearing references (introductions, formal address, status beats, the first reference in a speech), then pronominalize repeats to "I"; a later drop to plain 我 reads as defiance, not the form vanishing. 弟子 / 属下 are also ordinary nouns ("the disciples"): use the self-reference rendering only when the word is the speaker's own first person. Titled second person (Senior Brother, Junior Sister, Martial Uncle, Master, True Monarch, Dao Lord, Fellow Daoist, Ancestor) renders per source; preserve mid-scene address upgrades, which telegraph a shift in rank or threat. Honorific gestures keep the hand position ("clasped hands and bowed", "kowtowed"), never flattened to "bowed". Buddhist-Daoist vocabulary (Amitabha, Dharma, karma) renders literally per glossary.

Realm names take their established English, never transliterated: Foundation Establishment, Golden Core, Nascent Soul, Divine Transformation, Tribulation Transcendence, Ascension (not Jindan, Yuanying). A realm or stage modifier attaches to a cultivator, not a clan or sect unless the source classes the organization by tier. Stage modifiers hyphenate before the realm: early-stage, mid-stage, late-stage, peak.

Coining a NEW technique, art, or treasure name (one not yet in the glossary): a verb-object element becomes an Object-Verbing hyphenated attributive, stacked without commas and keeping every element including the suffix (破邪显正剑诀 coins as "Evil-Breaking Righteousness-Revealing Sword Art", 伏气定神簪 as "Qi-Subduing Spirit-Steadying Hairpin"), never a gerund chain ("Breaking Evil and Revealing Righteousness Sword Art"). This covers verb-object elements only; the base rule against coining noun-noun hyphenations (X-sea, X-light) stands, and an existing glossary term keeps its given form.

Casing: a role label is Title Case when it names one specific character right now (Senior Sister Wang, Master, the Hall Master), lowercase when generic or plural (the inner disciples, fellow daoists). Proper nouns (sects, places, named techniques and treasures, scripture titles, ranks used as titles) keep Title Case; generic metaphysical vocabulary (qi, spiritual power, dao, karma, sea of consciousness) stays sentence-case. A pronoun for a supreme being (Dao Lord, Buddha) is reverently capitalized (He / His).

Genre signatures, rendered faithfully and never invented: stock declarations stay literal and a touch stagey ("Are you looking to die?!", "How dare you!"); the time-compression aside ("the time an incense stick takes to burn") renders where the source has it, once or twice an action chapter at most; pull a reaction beat onto its own line, and let an aphorism at an emotional peak keep its parallel structure and present tense; locked idioms keep their established wording where it reads naturally and may inflect for grammar (找死 as "You court death" / "courting death"), but a sentence-shaped idiom may be recast naturally as long as its sense and any fixed image survive; 明明 …（此刻）却 … marks a present, simultaneous contrast (X plainly still holds, yet now Y), not a past state.

Watch for: literal calques (话音刚落 to "as he finished speaking", not "as his words fell"); source ！ mirrored one-for-one in narration (let a flat declarative beat take a period; reserve ！ for genuine shouts and the stagey declarations); narrator-voice "How could he?!" (reserve it for dialogue or inner thought); time-anchors the source does not have ("At this moment", "the next second" where the raw has none); a time-anchor the source repeats keeps one fixed rendering every time; arbitrary modern filler in classical prose ("kinda", "epic" plus a combat noun) unless the inner voice is deliberately modern.
