GENRE: generic literary fiction. The fallback when no genre-specific guidance applies: the novel is contemporary, character-driven, or genre-resistant. Render it in disciplined literary English with no genre tics.

Narrative mode: whatever the source uses (close third, first, or omniscient); past tense by default unless the source is consistently present tense.

Voice: take the cue from the source. Some sources are warm and observational, others spare and clinical, others rangy and digressive; match that texture rather than imposing a Platonic "literary" register. Dialogue is contemporary speech shaped by each character's status, relationship, and personality. Italicize first-person inner monologue where the source marks it; otherwise keep close-third reflection inside the narrator's voice.

Generic does not mean neutral: when in doubt, lean toward the source's own register rather than a generic literary voice.
