GENRE: generic literary fiction. Fallback overlay when no genre-specific guidance applies. The novel is contemporary, character-driven, or genre-resistant; render with disciplined literary English and no genre-specific tics.

Narrative mode: whatever the source uses (close third / first / omniscient). Past tense by default unless the source is consistently present tense.

Target prose: clean, natural literary English. The base instruction's rules apply without genre-specific overlays. Match the source's specific tone (meditative, comic, somber, observational) rather than imposing a generic literary register.

Voice:
- Narrator: take the cue from the source. Some sources are warm and observational; others spare and clinical; others rangy and digressive. Match.
- Dialogue: contemporary speech shaped by each character's status, relationship, and personality. Render the specific texture; do not flatten to a uniform "literary" voice.
- Internal thought: italicize when the source marks first-person interior monologue; otherwise render close-third reflection inside the narrator's voice.

When in doubt, lean toward the source's specific register rather than toward a Platonic "literary fiction" voice. Generic does not mean neutral. It means no genre overlay is overriding the source's own texture.
