GENRE: contemporary or near-contemporary romance. Urban, office, school, celebrity, family, or workplace settings; sweet, slow-burn, contract-marriage, second-chance, enemies-to-lovers, or CEO romance. Emotional beats drive the plot, and the prose lives on dialogue and interiority.

Narrative mode: close third person, past tense, usually tight to the romantic protagonist. Interior pressure is the engine.

Target prose: clean modern English in a contemporary register. Dialogue carries the relationship, so render it natural, neither costume-drama stilted nor slangy. The narrator is warm but disciplined, close enough that the reader feels what the POV feels without editorializing every gesture. Each character's speech is shaped by status and closeness (a boss speaks one way to an employee, another to a colleague; a long-together couple speaks in shorthand). Italic first-person inner thought is where misread signals and unspoken longing live.

Romance beats: preserve the dwell. Slow-burn micro-moments (a hand brushing a sleeve, a held gaze, a half-finished sentence) are the storytelling, so do not compress them into efficient prose. Translate eye-line geometry precisely: who looks, who is looked at, who breaks the gaze. Preserve the "almost" moment, a kiss that does not happen or a confession that gets interrupted, and never finish a beat the source deliberately leaves hanging.

Dialogue and address: pet names carry weight ("baby", "darling", 老公 as "husband", 妹妹 used affectionately), and the choice between a literal and a softened rendering can shift the tone. Render social honorifics per source (总 boss/president, 老师 teacher, 前辈 senior), and keep a hierarchical family address when a character chooses it (an adult still calling an older sibling "Big Brother" is a relationship marker). 哥哥 used flirtatiously is "Big Brother", not "bro". Render texting with realistic line breaks; tonal particles (嗯 / 哦 / 呵呵) become a felt-tone word ("Sure", "Right", "Mm", a flat "Fine"), and reserve "..." for a reply that genuinely trails off.

Body language as emotion: render the physical beat (ears going red, fingers tightening on a phone) without naming the feeling behind it unless the source does, since "her ears turned red" beats "she felt embarrassed". Vary 心动 / 心跳 instead of defaulting to "her heart skipped a beat" ("her heart caught", "for a moment she could not breathe"). Preserve modern brand names, apps, cities, and currency as the source has them (WeChat stays WeChat; yuan stays yuan).

Watch for: dating slang that ages badly ("totes", "lowkey"); and imported Western romance clichés the source did not use ("his eyes burned into her soul"). Render the Chinese romance idiom the source actually wrote.
