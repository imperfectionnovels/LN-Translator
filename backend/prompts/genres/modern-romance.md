GENRE: contemporary or near-contemporary romance. Urban, office, school, celebrity / entertainment industry, family, or workplace settings. Emotional beats drive the plot; the prose lives or dies on dialogue and interiority. Subgenres include sweet romance, slow-burn, contract marriage, second-chance, enemies-to-lovers, CEO romance.

Narrative mode: close third-person, past tense. The POV usually stays tight to the romantic protagonist; interior pressure is the engine.

Target prose: clean modern English. Contemporary register. Dialogue carries the relationship: render it natural, not period-stylized. Avoid both stilted formality (this is not a costume drama) and overly slangy register (the relationship beats should land cleanly).

Voice register:
- Narrator: warm but disciplined. Close enough to the POV's emotional state that the reader feels what she feels, restrained enough not to editorialize on every gesture.
- External dialogue: contemporary speech, shaped by each character's status and relationship to the speaker. A boss speaks differently to an employee than to a colleague; a long-term couple speaks in shorthand. Render those distinctions.
- Internal thought: italicized when the source marks first-person interior monologue. Often the place where misread signals, suppressed feelings, and unspoken longing live.

Romance-specific beats:
- Slow-burn micro-moments: a hand brushing a sleeve, a held gaze, a half-finished sentence. The source often dwells; the English should preserve the dwell. Do not compress emotional beats into efficient prose; the lingering IS the storytelling.
- "He looked at her, and she looked away": the choreography of who is looking, who is being looked at, who breaks the gaze. Translate these eye-line beats precisely; the romantic tension is in the geometry.
- The "almost" moment: a kiss that doesn't happen, a confession that gets interrupted, an answer left unsaid. Preserve the source's restraint; don't accidentally finish the beat the source deliberately leaves hanging.

Dialogue conventions:
- Pet names and nicknames carry weight. "Baby," "darling," "old man" (老公 / "husband"), "wifey" (老婆 / "wife"), "little sister" (妹妹 used affectionately): translate per glossary; the choice between literal and softened renderings can shift the relationship's tone.
- Honorifics matter less than in xianxia but still carry social signal. Boss / president (总 / 老板), professor / teacher (老师), senior (前辈): render per source.
- Family hierarchy address: "Mom" / "Dad" / "Older Brother": translate to the natural English equivalent, but preserve the hierarchical address when the source character uses it (e.g. an adult character still calling an older sibling "Big Brother" reads as a relationship marker, not just family).
- Texting and messaging: render IM exchanges with realistic line breaks and natural phrasing. Don't overformalize. If the source uses "嗯" / "哦" / "呵呵", these are tonal, not literal "mm" / "oh" / "haha." Render the felt tone (often "..." or "Sure" or "Right").

Body language and emotion:
- Render reactions as physical beats: blushes, looking down, fingers tightening on a phone, ears going red. Do not narrate the emotion behind the gesture unless the source does. "Her ears turned red" is more romantic than "she felt embarrassed."
- Translation of 心动 / 心跳 (heart-stirring, heartbeat): do NOT default to "her heart skipped a beat" as a cliché. Vary: "her heart caught," "for a moment she could not breathe," "something turned over inside her." Match the source's specific image where possible.

Settings:
- Modern brand names, technology, social media: preserve as the source has them. If the source mentions WeChat, render WeChat (or "the messaging app" if obscure); do not domesticate to WhatsApp. If the source mentions a specific city, university, or company, preserve.
- Currency: yuan / RMB stay yuan / RMB unless the glossary has chosen otherwise.

Watch-list:
- Avoid contemporary slang that dates the prose ("totes," "lit," "lowkey"). Use natural conversational English that will age reasonably.
- Do NOT impose Western romance-novel clichés the source did not use ("his eyes burned into her soul," "a thrill ran down her spine"). The Chinese romance idiom palette is its own; render what the source actually says.
- Honorific romance vocabulary: 哥哥 (older brother, used flirtatiously by a younger woman to a slightly older man): preserve the dynamic but render naturally. "Big brother" is correct; "bro" is not.
- Tense slips: contemporary romance loves the wistful past-tense reflection. Keep tense consistent within a paragraph; if the POV reflects mid-paragraph, that reflection still uses simple past unless the clause is a universal truth.
