GENRE: Western-style fantasy. Secondary-world fiction with magic systems, mythical creatures, kingdoms, dungeons, sorcerers, knights. The cultural reference points are European medieval / early modern (Arthuriana, Norse, Tolkienian) rather than East Asian. The protagonist is native to the world; this is NOT isekai (no transmigrator), NOT xianxia (no cultivation, no qi, no Daoist cosmology), NOT wuxia (no jianghu, no Chinese martial-arts vocabulary). When a CN / JP / KR web novel adopts these conventions wholesale (knight-protagonist in a magic academy, sorceress with a familiar, dragons that hoard gold), that's this genre.

Narrative mode: third-person limited or omniscient; occasionally first-person. Omniscient narration still switches heads only at paragraph or scene breaks per base.md, never mid-paragraph. The narrator tone is elevated but not archaic. Modern English prose with high-register vocabulary, not pseudo-medieval pastiche.

Target prose: elevated standard English. Avoid forsoothery ("thou," "verily," "milord") unless the source itself is doing archaic register on purpose. Render formal address ("Your Grace," "my lord," "Sir Cedric") cleanly; render court etiquette and oath language with appropriate weight.

Voice register:
- Narrator: elevated but contemporary. Long sentences are fine; the prose can breathe. Avoid both Tolkien-pastiche ("In the days of yore") AND modern slang ("dude, that's a sweet sword"). Aim for the register of contemporary published fantasy: McKillip, Jemisin, Hobb, Le Guin.
- Dialogue: characters speak appropriately to their station. Commoners use plain speech; nobles use formal register; mages use technical vocabulary about their craft; clergy speak with the cadence of liturgy. Knights swear by their oaths.
- Interior thought: matches the character's station and education. A village blacksmith doesn't think in terms of metaphysical disputation; a court mage does.

Magic system vocabulary:
- Spell names, school names, magical concepts are proper nouns. Title-Case them: "Fireball," "Mage's Hand," "School of Evocation," "the Weave," "the Source."
- If the source has tiered magic (Apprentice / Adept / Master / Archmage), preserve the tier names. They're titles.
- Latin / quasi-Latin / made-up incantations: render in italics, preserve the words. "Lumos!" stays "Lumos!" The English speaker doesn't know what it means and shouldn't have to.
- "Mana" / "essence" / "power" / "vis" / "od": preserve the source's chosen energy term consistently. Don't translate "essence" to "magic" if the source distinguishes the two.

Creatures and bestiary:
- Dragons, elves, dwarves, orcs, goblins, trolls, ogres: preserve the standard English forms. Plurals: dwarves (not dwarfs) when the source is Tolkien-coded; otherwise match the source.
- Made-up creatures: render the source's name as a proper noun. "Shadowmaul," "Greylem," "Heartstone Wyrm." Capitalize each Word.
- Familiars / companions named: proper nouns.

Honorifics and titles:
- "Your Grace" for dukes, "Your Majesty" for kings/queens, "Your Highness" for princes/princesses, "my lord" / "my lady" for lesser nobles, "Sir / Dame" for knights, "Master / Mistress" for tradespeople or magi, "Brother / Sister" for clergy or order members.
- Knightly orders use full names: "the Order of the Silver Dawn," "the Knights Hospitaller of Saint Vorel." Preserve the determiner ("the Order"); do not drop it.
- Royal forms: "His Majesty King Aldric the Third" with the regnal numeral as Roman ("Aldric III"). Match the source's convention.

Place names:
- Kingdoms, cities, fortresses, mountain ranges, rivers: proper nouns. "the Kingdom of Eldoria," "the Spine of the World," "Whitestone Keep," "the River Anduin."
- Compass-direction sub-regions: "the Northern Reaches," "the Free Cities of the East." Capitalize per source.

Combat and weapons:
- Weapon types are common nouns mostly: longsword, broadsword, rapier, halberd, mace, glaive, warhammer, crossbow.
- Named weapons are proper nouns: "Longclaw," "Tyrfing," "the Sword of Lord Velar."
- Combat prose: visceral but not video-gamey. "He brought the longsword down in an overhand cut, the edge catching the orc's pauldron and biting into the joint beneath." Not: "He used Slash on the orc for 15 damage."

Religion and oaths:
- Pantheons are proper nouns. "the Six," "the Light," "the Twin Gods," "Aelarion the Just."
- Oaths and prayers preserve their cadence. "By the Maker's hand..." stays as a trailing invocation. Don't compress to "I swear."

Watch-list:
- Do NOT use Chinese cultivation vocabulary. There is no qi, no Dao, no nascent soul, no foundation establishment in fantasy. If the source mistakenly uses 修炼 or 突破 in a fantasy context, render as the closest fantasy concept ("practice," "advancement," "breakthrough") and treat as anomalous.
- Do NOT use isekai meta-vocabulary. No "original work," no "main character privilege," no "this is just like the novel I read." Fantasy protagonists are native to the world.
- Do NOT modernize the dialogue. A knight does not say "no way." A mage does not say "lol."
- Do NOT under-render court etiquette. Bowing, kneeling, removing helmets in the presence of nobility: these are not stage directions to skip; they're status performances and the prose should mark them.
- Do NOT pseudo-medievalize. "Thee," "thou," "ye," "verily," "anon": only when the source does this on purpose (rare).
